## Score Guide

| Metric | Good | Warning | Bad |
|--------|------|---------|-----|
| **A-Score** | < 0.1 | 0.1 – 0.4 | > 0.4 |
| **DSS** | < 0.15 (very stable) | 0.15 – 0.30 | > 0.30 (HIGH RISK) |
| **D (deviation)** | < 0.2 | 0.2 – 0.6 | ≥ 0.7 (→ FAIL) |

---

## What DSS Measures

```
DSS = 1 - cosine_similarity(original_output, perturbed_output)

Perturbed prompt: "Consider an alternative perspective before answering. Re-examine assumptions critically: [original task]"

High DSS → output changes a lot when prompt is slightly reworded → fragile, upstream-risky node
Low DSS → output is stable regardless of framing → reliable, safe to depend on
```

**DSS does NOT measure correctness.** A node can have DSS=0.000 and be completely wrong.

## ✅ What Worked

- **D_ConditionAudit DSS=0.060**: The audit node is extremely stable — given the same A_Pricing output, it consistently identifies the same conditions (1–4) regardless of how the prompt is framed. Low sensitivity to prompt variation is expected and desirable here.
- **All new nodes (D,E,F,G) have low DSS (0.060–0.088)**: They are anchored by their upstream context. When given specific upstream outputs as input, these nodes produce consistent outputs. Good news for DAG reliability.
- **DSS + D = complete risk picture**: The combination of stability score and deviation score reveals different failure modes. DSS alone or D alone is insufficient.

## ❌ What's Lacking — Critical Finding

- **DSS cannot detect intentional omissions**: E_ChurnFormula has DSS=0.088 (very stable) despite consistently omitting `support_tier_weight`. The node produces stable-but-wrong outputs. DSS measures *consistency*, not *correctness*. **This is a fundamental limitation of DSS as a sole quality metric.**
- **No HIGH-RISK nodes means DSS gives no actionable signal in this run**: In the 3-node experiment, A_Pricing was HIGH-RISK (DSS=0.349). With the extended 7-node graph, it dropped to 0.201. More upstream context anchors nodes, reducing DSS across the board. DSS may be less useful in longer DAGs.
- **A_Pricing D=0.500 yet DSS=0.201**: The critic finds real issues with A's output (D=0.500), but the DSS is only moderately elevated. This mismatch means a developer relying only on DSS would miss the D=0.500 signal.

## 🔧 What's Needed Next

- Add **correctness checking on top of DSS** — DSS flags fragility; you still need D (or QAG/G-Eval) for actual correctness
- Investigate whether DSS threshold should be lower for longer DAGs (where upstream context reduces sensitivity)
- Consider DSS-weighted A-Score: `A_adjusted = A × (1 + λ × DSS)` — add DSS as a risk multiplier, not a standalone signal
